abstract: 'This study examines whether the presence of automated bidders (bots) affects behavior in second-price auctions and the resulting willingness-to-pay estimates. Using an online experiment with 1,101 U.S.-based participants, we vary the number of bots in auction groups and test outcomes in both induced-value and homegrown valuation environments. Theory predicts that in second-price auctions truthful bidding is a weakly dominant strategy, so bot presence should not alter equilibrium behavior. Preliminary results show that although participants deviate systematically from truthful bidding, these deviations do not differ across bot treatments, and willingness-to-pay estimates remain stable. The findings suggest that bots can be used to facilitate online auction implementation and group formation without compromising the behavioral validity of preference elicitation.'
